Background

3D Gaussian Splatting, in 90 seconds

Alpha-composited splatting
$$C=\sum_{i=1}^{N} c_i\,\alpha_i \prod_{j<i}(1-\alpha_j)$$

Gaussian primitive

Each element is ~60 floats: position $\boldsymbol{\mu}$, covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}=\mathbf{RSS}^\top\mathbf{R}^\top$, opacity $\alpha$, SH color. 1 M Gaussians ≈ 60 M free parameters, no topology.

Differentiable rasterizer

16×16 tiles, front-to-back sort, exact alpha compositing. Gradients flow end-to-end — this is the engine we keep.

Heuristic densification

Every 100 iters: clone, split, prune by view-space gradient statistics. Empirical, per-particle, no geometric guarantee.

✓ photorealistic✓ real-time✓ end-to-end gradients ✗ no surface✗ not editable✗ floaters

Splats are only ever correlated inside the rasterizer's 2D tile crop — a proxy for true geodesic proximity that fails at edges.

Problem

What 3DGS structurally cannot do

No topology

No mesh, no normals, no inside/outside. Collision, simulation, CAD export all need post-hoc extraction against noisy unstructured normals.

Not editable

No structural coupling between particles. Bending a wall means finding and moving thousands of Gaussians — then re-optimizing.

No resolution adaptation

Once trained, the particle count is frozen. A 3DGS model cannot meaningfully densify or coarsen at inference without retraining.

Prior work asks
"Given optimized Gaussians, how do we constrain them to approximate a surface?"
We invert the question
"Given a $C^2$ parametric surface, can we derive every splat attribute from its differential geometry?"
Key insight: real surfaces are smooth. One parametric surface replaces millions of correlated particles — and the result is editable, compact, and resolution-free.

Planar splats + multi-view photometric consistency. Strong geometry; still unstructured.

$S(u,v)$ is the primary learnable object. Position, rotation, scale of every Gaussian are consequences of surface differential geometry — no regularizer, no extraction, no post-hoc mesh.

Structure is not a constraint imposed on Gaussians — it is the representation itself.

Our method

The core idea: splats as differential geometry

Position — eval
$$\boldsymbol{\mu}(u,v)=S(u,v)$$
Rotation — tangent frame
$$\mathbf{R}=[\,\hat t_u \mid \hat t_v^{\perp} \mid \hat n\,],\qquad \hat n=\tfrac{\mathbf{S}_u\times \mathbf{S}_v}{\lVert \mathbf{S}_u\times \mathbf{S}_v\rVert}$$
Scale — first derivatives
$$\sigma_u=\lVert\mathbf{S}_u\rVert\,\Delta u,\quad \sigma_v=\lVert\mathbf{S}_v\rVert\,\Delta v,\quad \sigma_n=\epsilon$$

Learn one surface; position, orientation and footprint of every splat come free from $\partial S/\partial u,\ \partial S/\partial v$. Appearance (SH, opacity) is interpolated from control features with the same basis: $a(u,v)=\sum_{i,j}N_{i,p}N_{j,q}\,\varphi(\tilde a_{ij})$.

● t̂ᵤ● t̂ᵥ⊥ ● n̂◔ derived splat

The splat rides the surface — orientation is never a free parameter

Densification

Densification = knot insertion, not particle cloning

step through insertions — watch error collapse
— ground truth · — B-spline · ● control points

Where 3DGS clones particles…

…we refine the underlying surface. Boehm's knot insertion is exact — the surface is unchanged, expressivity grows where the error criterion demands it:

$\hat k=\arg\max_k \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal G_k|}\sum_{g\in\mathcal G_k}\bar\varepsilon_g$

Structure preserved by construction

New control points arrive as convex combinations of old ones (Boehm alphas) — no floaters can appear. Adam moments are spliced to the new parameter shape, so optimization never restarts.

3DGS densification is a gradient-statistics heuristic. Ours is a geometric criterion on a coherent manifold.
Method

The UV grid is the data structure

Visibility-driven UV sampling

Per-splat visibility from the rasterizer's out_observe. Every $K$ iterations, UV samples are re-placed by inverse-CDF of the visibility marginals — dense where cameras look, sparse where occluded. Constant splat budget, zero overhead.

bounded UV — no boundary degeneracy
$u=\epsilon+(1-2\epsilon)\varphi(\tilde u),\ \ \epsilon=0.01$

Immune to the Swiss-Roll problem

UV adjacency indexes geodesic neighbors. Points that are Euclidean-close but manifold-distant never become spurious neighbors — P[i±1, j] is structurally on-surface. Smoothness and coverage losses collapse to stride-1 array reads.

Meshing / neighborhood cost — n = splat count
O(V), V≫n Marching Cubes / Poisson

voxel grid over the whole bounding box · needs normals

O(n log n) Delaunay / Ball-pivoting

KD-tree lookups · unstructured output

O(n) · O(1) neighbors UV kernel scan (ours)

the mesh is the UV grid — no search, no hyperparameter

Every mesh, neighbor query, and regularizer is an array operation on the parametric grid.
Resolution

Fixed budget, infinite resolution

The surface is continuous — splat count is a render-time sampling choice, not a property of the model.

Zoom in — subdivide the UV crop

Restrict $(u,v)$ to the region of interest, keep the same $S\times S$ sample grid: each splat's geodesic footprint contracts. Detail rises at constant cost.

Zoom out — coarsen for free

Same surface rendered with fewer, larger splats for far views — a principled LOD ladder with no retraining and no extra storage.

A trained 3DGS model can do neither — its particle count is baked in at training time.

Emergent capability

3D segmentation through the 2D feature domain

Every Gaussian lives at a UV coordinate — a click in 2D feature space is already a view-independent 3D segment.

① click in feature space

A distilled feature field (DINO/CLIP) lives on the same UV grid. One click → thresholded similarity mask. No 3D clustering.

② lifts to 3D — roof

The UV mask selects exactly the Gaussians on that surface patch — coherent from views never used for the click.

③ complement — walls

Everything not selected is an equally sharp complementary region — same UV domain, one boundary.

Why 3DGS can't do this cleanly: without a shared 2D domain, features must be clustered in ℝ³ — noisy, view-inconsistent, blind to surface adjacency. Here, “segment” and “UV region” are the same object.

Analysis

Each design choice earns its place

Multilevel B-spline initialization

Random init: geometry losses meaningless until ~iter 7k. Multilevel B-spline prior: active at iter 150 — 50× earlier, and the only reason early normal supervision works at all.

Visibility-driven UV sampling

Uniform grid wastes splat budget on occluded UV regions. Inverse-CDF re-placement keeps the budget where cameras actually look — at zero memory overhead.

Adaptive knot insertion

Fixed knot grid under-resolves high-curvature regions. Error-driven Boehm insertion tracks curvature demand; exactness means densification never destabilizes training.

NURBS weights + residual scaling

Next experiment. Both disabled in the 18.3 dB ablation. Full config adds conic precision and per-splat opacity range — the hypothesis: geometry doesn't need them, photometric quality does.

18.3 dB is the floor — 1/20th training, two features off. The ablation order makes explicit what each knob controls.
Limitations

What this doesn't do yet — and what's next

Known & disclosed

Single-patch topology

One B-spline patch per surface component, fixed before training. A hole, or two touching objects, isn't representable — the multi-surface extension is future work, not solved today.
